How does one learn? Here are some of my thoughts on the matter.

As babies, the entire world is unknown to us. We try to make sense of the world by identifying patterns, learning by trial-and-error, and learning from examples. To relate this to computer science, these techniques can be analogized to techniques in machine learning. Does that mean we are machines? Not exactly, but I digress.

Past early infancy, we mainly rely on our parents and other adults to learn more about how the world works. If one is privileged enough, education becomes the mainstay for intellectual growth.

Much of my own experience is through the American public education system; however, extracurricular activities involving sports, music lessons, and vacations lend much to be learned from.

The identification of patterns continues to be the principal method of learning, whether it be through scientific-means or pursuits that are more social in nature. We learn that, in general, certain actions have certain consequences and as a result, we form these rulesets in our minds about how the world works, continuing to add more and more rules and exceptions to rules, addendums, footnotes, and so on...

We rely on these patterns for two main reasons:

    1. Efficiency
    2. Comfort

As human beings, we want to be able to experience life to its fullest, but, initially, we believe that we are constrained by one major factor: time. We believe that we have a limited amount of time (disregarding Jesus and his promise). Thus, we want to be able to do things as fast as possible, rather as efficiently as possible.

However, as human beings, we also are able to feel pain, and generally, we wish to avoid pain. Thus, the desire for comfort plays a role in our decision-making and how we go about our lives. No one wants to be uncomfortable. (But sometimes the best things happen outside of our comfort zone).

Through schooling, we learn tried and true techniques about how to study academic subjects, but also it provides a sandbox for social interaction, to learn about how we interact with each other. Barring non-social interactions with the world, we can see how important schooling is to the development of growing children and adolescents.

As topics become more and more complex, paths diverge, and students (perhaps) (and with guidance) begin to choose the area (in life) to pursue. Social work, govenment, industry, academia, health, just to name a few. Some of the less fortunate, however, learn that in this world, money is a driving factor and provides privilege and opportunity. Some may discover God, and others may utterly reject Him.

Learning continues into early adulthood. Though, being a young adult myself, I shall temporarily halt here. There are many aspects of life that are yet unknown to me, but I suspect that although we still rely on the methods of learning from childhood, new experiences and wonders may cause us to rethink how we learn and what we are learning--about the world, about each other, and about ourselves.
